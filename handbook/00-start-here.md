## The deal

You have an AI assistant that can write code, explain errors, and answer questions instantly. That's genuinely powerful — and it comes with a subtle trap:

> **The assistant can make your *work* look better while leaving *you* no better.**

You can hand in a clean analysis you don't really understand, or a figure you couldn't make again on your own. None of that builds the thing you're actually here for this summer — **becoming a better scientist.**

So the deal is simple:

> **Use the assistant to learn faster, never to think less.**

The goal isn't to avoid AI. It's to use it the way a good mentor would: clearing dead-ends and explaining things, while leaving the real thinking — the part that makes *you* better — to you.

> **In the RMBL Undergraduate Program?** It has its own AI rules you must follow — most importantly: **do your R coding workshop assignments AI-free** (on your own, until they're turned in), **don't let AI write your prose, and cite any AI use.** See [`05-rmbl-ed-policy.md`](05-rmbl-ed-policy.md) before you start.

## Why this matters

When a tool will hand you the answer instantly, it's tempting to stop trying to work it out yourself. Do that too often and three things quietly go wrong (there's real research behind this — see [`reference/evidence-base.md`](../reference/evidence-base.md)):

- **You feel like you understand more than you do.** Watching a clear explanation is not the same as being able to produce it. It's easy to nod along and then be stuck on your own.
- **You skip the part where learning happens.** Being a little stuck, and working through it, is exactly what makes knowledge stick. Hand that off and it doesn't.
- **You can't tell when the AI is wrong.** It is confidently wrong on a regular basis. Spotting that only comes from having done the work yourself.

The fix isn't willpower — it's a few simple **habits** that keep you in the driver's seat by default. That's what the handbook gives you.

---

## Three principles

### 1. You are the scientist. The AI is the assistant.
You own the question, the plan, and the call on whether the result is right. The assistant handles boilerplate, syntax, and first drafts you check. **Never let it own the thinking.**

### 2. Try it first.
Attempt the problem before you ask. Write your plan before you ask for code. A few minutes of being stuck is not wasted — it's where the learning is. (The "10-minute rule" is in [`03-working-with-the-assistant.md`](03-working-with-the-assistant.md).)

### 3. If you can't explain it, you don't ship it.
The bar for any code or result going into your project: **you can explain what it does, and why it's right, to a labmate — without the AI.** If you can't yet, that's a to-do, not a finished result. Ask the assistant to *teach* you the missing piece, then explain it back.

## What this looks like day-to-day

| Instead of... | Do this |
|---------------|---------|
| "Write me code to analyze this data." | "Here's my plan: [plan]. Does this look right? Then help me with the first step." |
| Pasting an error and running whatever it says | "Explain what this error means and what's likely causing it." Then *you* fix it. |
| Accepting a long function you didn't read | Read it. Ask about any line you don't get. Then keep it — or simplify it. |
| "Is this right?" → trust the answer | Check it against something real: a known case, a quick calculation, a sanity plot. |

## How you know it's working

At the end of the summer, the question isn't "how much did you produce?" It's:

> **Can you do more, and understand more, than you could in May — *without* the AI in front of you?**

If yes, you used it right. That's the whole game.
