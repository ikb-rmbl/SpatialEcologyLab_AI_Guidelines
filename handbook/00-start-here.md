# 00 — Start Here

Read this once. It's the whole philosophy in ten minutes. Everything else in the handbook is just the detail.

---

## The deal

You have an AI assistant that can write code, explain errors, and answer questions instantly. That is genuinely powerful. It is also a trap, and the trap is subtle:

> **The assistant can make your *work* better while making *you* worse.**

You can ship a clean analysis you don't understand. You can fix a bug you couldn't have found. You can produce a figure you couldn't reproduce. None of that builds the thing you're actually here to build this summer — **your own judgment as a scientist.**

So the lab's deal is simple:

> **Use the assistant to learn faster, never to think less.**

The goal is not to avoid AI. It's to use it the way a good mentor uses it with a student: to remove dead-ends and accelerate understanding, while leaving the actual thinking — the part that makes you better — to you.

> **In the RMBL Undergraduate Program?** That program has its own AI policy that sets rules you must follow — most importantly: **don't let AI write your prose, formally cite any AI use, and keep sensitive data out of AI tools.** Read **[`05-rmbl-ed-policy.md`](05-rmbl-ed-policy.md)** for how the lab's approach fits with it — including the lab's position on letting the assistant see your *open* workspace data — before you start.

## The failure mode we're avoiding: cognitive surrender

When a tool will instantly give you an answer, the tempting move is to stop trying to produce the answer yourself. Do this enough and a few things happen, and they're backed by real research (see [`reference/evidence-base.md`](../reference/evidence-base.md)):

- **You feel like you understand more than you do.** Watching a fluent explanation creates an *illusion of competence*. You nod along; you couldn't reproduce it.
- **You stop practicing the hard part.** The struggle of generating an answer yourself — being a little stuck — is *exactly* the thing that builds durable understanding. Skip it and the learning doesn't stick.
- **Your judgment doesn't develop.** The assistant is confidently wrong on a regular basis. If you can't tell when, you can't use it safely — and that ability only comes from having done the work yourself.

We call the slide into this "cognitive surrender." The antidote isn't willpower. It's **work patterns** that keep you in the driver's seat by default. That's what this handbook gives you.

## Three principles

### 1. You are the scientist. The AI is the assistant.
You own the question, the plan, the interpretation, and the final call on whether the code is right. The assistant owns boilerplate, syntax lookups, and first drafts you will scrutinize. **Never let it own the thinking.**

### 2. Struggle a little first.
Try the problem before you ask. Write the plan before you ask for code. Predict the answer before you run it. A few minutes of productive struggle is not wasted time — it's the part where learning actually happens. (Details and the "10-minute rule" in [`03-working-with-the-assistant.md`](03-working-with-the-assistant.md).)

### 3. If you can't explain it, you don't ship it.
The bar for any code or result entering your project: **you can explain what it does and why it's correct to a labmate, without the AI.** If you can't, that's not a finished result — it's a to-do item. Ask the assistant to *teach* you the part you're missing, then explain it back.

## What this looks like day-to-day

| Instead of... | Do this |
|---------------|---------|
| "Write me code to analyze this data." | "Here's my plan: [plan]. Does this approach have problems? Then help me write the first step." |
| Pasting an error and running whatever it says | "Explain what this error means and what's likely causing it." Then *you* fix it. |
| Accepting a 40-line function you don't read | Read it. Ask about any line you don't understand. Then keep it — or rewrite it simpler. |
| "Is this right?" → trust the answer | Verify against something real: a known case, a hand calculation, a sanity plot. |

## The test for whether you're doing it right

At the end of the summer, the question isn't "how much did you produce?" It's:

> **Can you do more, and understand more, than you could in May — *without* the AI in front of you?**

If yes, you used it right. That's the whole game.
